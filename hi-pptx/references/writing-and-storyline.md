## Decision-backward planning

Start with the meeting outcome:

1. What should the audience understand?
2. What should they decide, approve, challenge, or do?
3. What evidence is required for that outcome?
4. What context is necessary before the evidence makes sense?
5. What risks, assumptions, or dependencies could block the outcome?

Draft the last content slide first, then build the shortest credible path to it.

A common client storyline is:

`Context → Observed issue → Implication → Proposed response → How it works → Plan → Risks/assumptions → Decision/next step`

Do not force this sequence when the meeting has a different purpose.

## Slide purpose

Assign one primary purpose:

- Orient.
- Explain.
- Compare.
- Diagnose.
- Recommend.
- Decide.
- Plan.
- Confirm.

If a slide serves several purposes, split it or make one purpose visibly dominant.

## Takeaway titles

Write a complete, specific thought:

- Weak: “Current Situation”
- Better: “Three manual handoffs extend the response cycle”

- Weak: “Our Solution”
- Better: “A bounded pilot can test value without changing the core workflow”

- Weak: “Next Steps”
- Better: “Confirm scope and owners before beginning discovery”

Use sentence case. Avoid title punctuation unless it helps meaning. Do not use slogans such as “Unlocking the Future” or “Revolutionizing the Industry.”

## Body writing

- Use concrete nouns and active verbs.
- Keep bullets parallel.
- Put the key point first.
- Limit most bullet lists to 3–5 items.
- Use fragments when they scan better than full sentences.
- Avoid repeating the title in the body.
- Remove “in order to,” “leverage,” “world-class,” “seamless,” and other filler unless they carry specific meaning.
- Put detailed explanation in notes or an appendix.

## Evidence language

Use explicit labels:

- `Actual` for verified observed data.
- `Target` for an approved goal.
- `Proposed` for a recommendation.
- `Assumption` for an unverified planning premise.
- `Illustrative` for a fictional example.
- `TBD` for missing evidence.

Do not imply precision that the source does not support. Preserve units, dates, sample sizes, and source context.

## Japanese-customer-facing tone

- Establish background and scope before making a recommendation.
- Replace certainty with calibrated language when evidence is incomplete.
- Prefer “We propose validating…” to “This will guarantee…”.
- Present both benefit and operational condition.
- Show owners and decision points.
- Avoid public criticism of current teams or systems.
- Use bilingual terminology consistently when bilingual content is requested.
- Verify Japanese fonts by rendering on the target environment.

## Storyline review

Read only the slide titles in sequence. They should form a coherent argument without body text.

Then ask:

- Does each slide answer a question created by the previous slide?
- Is the recommendation supported before it is requested?
- Are risks and dependencies shown before commitment?
- Are duplicate slides saying the same thing?
- Could any slide move to the appendix without harming the meeting?
- Is the final ask specific enough to act on?
